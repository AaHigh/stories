Sinners on
Operation No Fly Zone

Local law enforcement success, state-level scrutiny, and federal enforcement in California's Central Valley.

Compiled from public records & reported events
No statements invented • Grounded in verifiable sources

In the spring of 2022, Fresno saw one of the largest coordinated law enforcement operations in its recent history. Called Operation No Fly Zone, it targeted violent street gangs — particularly the Flyboys and Hoover 107 — linked to multiple homicides, drive-by shootings, and human trafficking.

The months-long investigation involved the Fresno Police Department, Fresno County Sheriff’s Office, Fresno County District Attorney’s Office, California Department of Justice, FBI, Homeland Security Investigations, ATF, and other agencies. It ended with dozens of felony arrests, federal firearms charges against several defendants, and the disruption of planned violence.

“Today, Fresno is safer thanks to collaboration, determination, and swift action. No family should ever have to worry about drive-bys or other forms of gun violence in the neighborhoods where their children live and play… Together, we’re putting public safety first.”
— Then-Attorney General Rob Bonta, 2022

Fresno County District Attorney Lisa A. Smittcamp, a prosecutor with deep roots in the county who has built her reputation on cases involving gangs, domestic violence, child exploitation, and later fentanyl trafficking, was part of that local effort. Her office has consistently emphasized maximum accountability for violent and predatory offenders.

In later fentanyl-related prosecutions, she publicly stated that “fentanyl continues to claim lives in our community, and when someone knowingly sells this poison and a life is lost, we will pursue maximum accountability under the law.”

Those local and state-federal partnerships produced concrete results against what Smittcamp’s office and local police described as the “sinners” — the repeat violent actors and traffickers operating in the community.

State-Level Scrutiny Emerges

By 2025–2026, a different set of stories had moved into public view involving California’s Attorney General Rob Bonta and his wife, Assemblymember Mia Bonta. Federal investigations into alleged bribery and corruption in Oakland and the East Bay centered on figures with past political and financial ties to the Bontas.

Reporting documented returned campaign contributions after FBI raids, substantial legal expenditures from Bonta’s campaign funds in connection with federal interviews (his team described him as a possible victim in the probe and not a close associate), and questions about past “behested” payments and nonprofit arrangements connected to his wife. Mia Bonta’s role chairing a legislative budget subcommittee that oversees her husband’s Department of Justice budget also drew ethics commentary, even though it was not illegal.

These stories moved from political niche coverage into wider public consciousness. They contributed to broader conversations about influence, self-dealing, and trust in Sacramento institutions. Some Democrats and observers began voicing concerns about patterns that went beyond normal political friction.

Federal Enforcement Advances

At the same time, the federal government under the second Trump administration launched Operation Metro Surge in Minnesota beginning in late 2025. Described by DHS as its largest immigration enforcement operation, it deployed thousands of agents to the Twin Cities, produced thousands of arrests, triggered lawsuits from Minnesota and local governments, and included temporary flight restrictions around Department of Homeland Security vehicles and facilities (later modified by the FAA following legal challenges by journalists).

By mid-2026, ICE arrest numbers in California’s Central Valley had risen sharply — reported as a 58% increase in the first seven months compared with the prior year — with corresponding increases in deportations from Fresno County and surrounding areas. The enforcement emphasis on individuals with criminal histories, including those connected to trafficking and gang activity, overlapped thematically with the targets of earlier operations like No Fly Zone.

The Gap and the Reckoning

In this environment, the gap between successful local multi-agency takedowns (where District Attorney Smittcamp’s office has been a consistent partner) and the accumulating state-level political scrutiny around the Attorney General’s office became part of public discussion.

Some residents and law enforcement voices argued that federal enforcement was stepping into areas where state political dynamics and sanctuary-style policies had created friction. Others raised concerns about overreach, due process, and the human costs of large-scale interior enforcement.

District Attorney Lisa Smittcamp has not, in the public record, directly addressed the Bonta controversies in the terms of a “champion of law enforcement” speech. Her public statements have remained focused on the work of her office: prosecuting serious crimes, protecting victims, and maintaining partnerships with local and federal law enforcement where those partnerships produce results against violent offenders and traffickers.

The larger story unfolding in the Central Valley is one of competing claims about who is actually delivering accountability.

  • Documented local successes against gangs and traffickers
  • Sustained questions — now in the public record — about influence and integrity at the state’s top law enforcement office
  • Aggressive federal operations that began in Minnesota and produced measurable increases in enforcement activity in Fresno and the broader region

Whether this amounts to a “public reckoning” depends on one’s perspective. What is clear from the record is that the old model of relying primarily on Sacramento for leadership on street-level crime and trafficking has lost some of its previous authority in the eyes of many who watch both the crime data and the political scandals.

Local prosecutors like Smittcamp, who built their careers in courtrooms rather than campaign committees, continue to operate in that gap — still doing the work of holding individual “sinners” accountable while the larger questions about institutional trust and which level of government is willing to act remain unresolved in the public square.
